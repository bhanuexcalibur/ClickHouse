Current gaps

Strong proof, thin positioning layer

ClickHouse has real financial services deployments. But the GTM motion still talks to engineers, not to the VP of Risk, the Head of Transaction Monitoring, or the CDO running a regulatory reporting stack.

  • Customer stories exist but speak to developers, not economic buyers
  • No published FS-specific solution brief, use-case page, or vertical pitch deck
  • Snowflake has a dedicated FS page, a partnership ecosystem, and co-sell relationships with major banks
  • Money20/20 Europe presence not yet established as a consistent anchor for the FS community
The build opportunity

A vertical motion that earns enterprise trust

Financial services buyers don't trust speed claims in a whitepaper. They trust benchmarks, reference customers in their sub-vertical, regulatory coverage language, and a vendor who shows up where they buy. That's what "from scratch" actually means here.

  • Segment FS into discrete buying groups: trading, payments, fraud/risk, compliance/regtech
  • Translate technical performance into business outcomes each segment cares about
  • Create credibility assets that shorten enterprise sales cycles
  • Build event and community presence anchored in Amsterdam and London

The core positioning bet: financial services companies don't need a faster database. They need a data infrastructure vendor that speaks fluent risk, compliance, and latency in the same sentence. ClickHouse can do that. The PMM job is to make that argument before the engineering team gets pulled into an RFP evaluation.

02 · Messaging Architecture

The same platform needs to land differently depending on who's in the room

ClickHouse's buyers in financial services span a wide authority stack. A quant engineer, a Head of Fraud, and a CDO evaluating infrastructure consolidation all have different trigger points. The message architecture below maps to each.

Audience Actual concern Message that lands
Quant / Trading Engineer Tick data at microsecond precision, ASOF joins, sub-second aggregations across billions of rows "ClickHouse was designed for time-series data at trading scale. ASOF joins, MergeTree indexing, columnar compression — no retrofitting required."
Head of Fraud / Risk Ops Detection latency, false positive rate, model retraining speed "Your fraud models are only as good as the data feeding them. ClickHouse ingests millions of events per second and makes them queryable without sampling."
CDO / Data Platform Lead Vendor consolidation, cost predictability, engineering overhead "One engine for observability, analytics, AI workloads, and event data. Mintlify cut cloud costs 60% and got 18x faster queries. That pattern holds in FS."
Compliance / RegTech Buyer Audit trails, data residency, query reproducibility "Full-fidelity historical data, not sampled snapshots. Every regulatory query runs on complete records, not approximations."
VP Engineering / CTO Developer experience, future-proofing, AI workload readiness "SQL with 100+ analytical functions. No proprietary query language. When your AI agents start generating queries 10x faster, the database doesn't blink."

Across all segments, the underlying message is consistent: most data infrastructure was built for a world where humans ran queries. AI agents and real-time detection systems changed the requirement. ClickHouse was built for the new requirement, not retrofitted for it.

04 · Competitive Landscape

How ClickHouse fits into conversations FS buyers are already having

FS data platform decisions rarely involve a single vendor comparison. Buyers are evaluating ClickHouse against their existing stack. The win condition is understanding what they're unhappy with and speaking to that first.

Vendor / Stack What FS teams use it for Where ClickHouse wins Where ClickHouse is challenged
ClickHouse Real-time analytics, fraud detection, trading analytics, observability Sub-second on billions of rows. Cost/GB. No sampling. SQL-native. AI workload ready. Smaller ecosystem than Snowflake. Less "enterprise managed" feel for non-technical buyers.
Snowflake Enterprise data warehousing, BI reporting, cross-cloud governance ClickHouse runs TPC-H SF100 at a fraction of the cost. Full workload at SF10 for under $0.01 (verified benchmark). Snowflake has deeper enterprise integrations and a larger certified partner ecosystem for FS.
Databricks ML/AI workloads, Spark pipelines, unified lakehouse Faster and cheaper on join-heavy analytical queries from 721M to 7.2B rows (verified benchmark). No Spark overhead. Databricks has deeper ML tooling and Unity Catalog for governance, relevant to model-heavy FS teams.
kdb+ (KX) High-frequency trading, tick databases, quant finance SQL familiarity over q/kdb proprietary language. Lower licensing cost. Easier to hire for. kdb+ has 30+ years on trading floors. Switching cost is high. The win here is greenfield, not replacement.
Elastic / Splunk Log observability, SIEM, security analytics ClickHouse compresses data to 1/12 to 1/20 of uncompressed size vs. Snowflake's ~4x (verified). At log scale, that's a material cost difference. Elastic and Splunk have pre-built FS compliance dashboards and SIEM integrations ClickHouse doesn't replicate out of the box.
Displacement narrative

When the conversation is "we already have Snowflake"

The right counter isn't "Snowflake is bad." It's: Snowflake was built for BI analysts running batch workloads. If your fraud team is running queries on billions of events and waiting, that's not a Snowflake configuration problem. That's an architecture problem. ClickHouse solves it without replacing the warehouse — it handles the workloads that saturate it.

Land and expand model

Where the wedge typically enters

In most FS deals, ClickHouse enters through a specific high-value workload: a fraud detection pipeline that can't run in the existing stack, a real-time trading dashboard that's throttling, or an observability layer drowning in Splunk costs. The expansion story — once a team is on ClickHouse for one workload — is consolidating adjacent ones onto the same infrastructure.

05 · Field Enablement

What the sales team needs to run a vertical play they didn't build

An industry marketing motion only creates value if field teams can actually run it. The content, talk tracks, and objection handling below are the PMM-to-field handoff.

talk track Opening the conversation with a CDO or data platform lead
▾

Most data infrastructure pitches open with performance benchmarks. CDOs have seen a hundred of those. The conversation that lands opens differently: "Your engineering team is probably already running some workloads on ClickHouse or evaluating it. I wanted to connect because the companies getting the most out of it in FS have found the real value isn't replacing what they have, it's handling what their existing stack can't."

From there, the questions that unlock the deal: What workloads feel slow or expensive? Where are engineers complaining about query latency? Are there analytical use cases deprioritized because the infrastructure makes them too hard?

objection handling "We're committed to Snowflake / Databricks for the next three years"
▾
"Our data team is standardized on Snowflake. We're not replacing it."
Good news: this isn't a replacement conversation. ClickHouse isn't a warehouse consolidation play — it's a real-time workload layer that sits alongside your existing stack. Deutsche Bank runs ClickHouse at enterprise scale without replacing their core warehouse. The workloads that are slow or expensive in Snowflake are exactly what ClickHouse is designed for.
"We need SOC 2, and data residency in EU."
ClickHouse Cloud is SOC 2 Type II certified. BYOC (Bring Your Own Cloud) supports data residency requirements in EU and APAC for regulated workloads. The AWS Financial Services Competency achieved March 2026 reflects the same controls financial services teams audit for.
"Your open source origins make procurement nervous."
Deutsche Bank and RBC are production customers. Capital One and Airwallex expanded their deployments last quarter. The open source core means no vendor lock-in and a 2,900+ contributor ecosystem. We go through the same enterprise procurement process as any other infrastructure vendor.
content assets The FS enablement kit — what to build in 90 days
▾

Field teams don't have time to synthesize technical docs into a discovery call. The assets they need:

  • One-pager by sub-vertical: trading analytics, fraud/risk, payments, regulatory reporting. Each leads with a customer result, not a feature list.
  • ROI calculator: inputs are current ingest volume, query frequency, and existing tool cost. Output is a cost comparison versus ClickHouse Cloud equivalent. This shortens the "we need to build a business case" step.
  • Discovery question bank: 10 to 15 questions segmented by buyer type that help reps identify the workloads where ClickHouse has the strongest advantage.
  • Battle cards: one per major competitor (Snowflake, Databricks, kdb+). Not a feature matrix — an objection-response card a rep can use in real time.
  • Reference intro scripts: approved templates for connecting a prospect to a customer peer in the same FS sub-vertical. Nothing closes a deal faster in enterprise than a peer reference.
event strategy Where FS buyers go, and what ClickHouse's presence should look like
▾

The Amsterdam location of this role isn't incidental. Money20/20 Europe runs in Amsterdam every June — it's the largest fintech event in Europe with 6,500+ executives and the right buyer density for this motion. Sibos (SWIFT's annual conference) brings the global banking and transaction infrastructure community together each autumn.

The event goal isn't booth traffic. It's pipeline generation through three mechanics:

  • A breakout session anchored by an FS customer speaking about their ClickHouse deployment — not a ClickHouse employee selling
  • Pre-event outreach using the vertical messaging framework to book meetings with target accounts before the show floor opens
  • A post-event content asset: a 1,500-word piece summarizing what data leaders were actually worried about, validated by in-person conversations
GTM iteration cadence

How to run the learning loop

  • Monthly win/loss debrief: structured conversation with field reps after every FS deal won or lost. The question isn't "what happened" — it's "what content or talk track would have changed the outcome."
  • Content signal tracking: which vertical one-pagers are being shared most by reps, which aren't being used. Non-usage is signal that the asset doesn't fit the conversation.
  • Quarterly hypothesis review: each sub-vertical GTM has one testable hypothesis. Track conversion rates by channel to validate or revise.
  • Customer expansion patterns: the expansion data tells you which workloads are winning the second land. Build the content that makes that pattern repeatable.
Metrics that matter

How to measure a vertical motion that's just starting

  • Pipeline sourced through vertical-specific content, tracked by UTM and deal tag
  • Sales cycle length for FS deals vs. non-FS: does vertical enablement compress time-to-decision?
  • Field adoption rate: what percentage of FS deals use the solution brief or battle card at least once?
  • NPS among FS accounts at 90 days and 12 months: expansion rate as the leading indicator of message fit
06 · 90-Day Plan

What building this from scratch actually looks like

The JD says "build from scratch." That's a different mandate than optimizing an existing motion. The first 90 days are about listening, mapping, and building the right foundation — not shipping content before knowing what the field actually needs.

Days 1 to 30 · Listen

Map the current state before building anything

  • Audit all existing FS customer stories, case studies, and references. Identify gaps by sub-vertical.
  • Ride along on 8 to 10 FS sales calls. Take notes on the objections that appear most often.
  • Interview 4 to 5 existing FS customers: what made them choose ClickHouse, what almost stopped them, what they wish they had in the evaluation.
  • Map the competitive landscape by sub-vertical: what are prospects displacing, and where does ClickHouse lose deals?
  • Identify the 3 highest-ARR FS logos and understand their expansion trajectory.
Days 31 to 60 · Build

First assets, first frameworks

  • Ship the first FS solution brief: one sub-vertical, validated with two field reps before publishing.
  • Draft the competitive battle cards (Snowflake, Databricks, kdb+). Run one battle card training with the EMEA field team.
  • Draft vertical messaging framework for all four FS segments. Share with product and field for validation.
  • Define the FS ICP more precisely: firmographic criteria, technographic signals, behavioral triggers.
  • Identify the right Money20/20 Europe strategy for the following year. Begin internal alignment.
Days 61 to 90 · Iterate

Measure, revise, and set the flywheel

  • First pipeline review: how are deals sourced through vertical content performing vs. baseline? Instrument the tracking.
  • Ship the FS ROI calculator. Pilot with 3 AEs. Gather feedback on whether it shortens the business case step.
  • Publish the first vertical-specific blog post or thought leadership piece, co-authored with a customer or field perspective.
  • Present the 90-day learnings to leadership with a proposed H2 roadmap: which sub-verticals to double down on, which partnerships to pursue, where the content gaps remain.

Why this sequence matters: an industry marketing motion built without listening to the field first produces assets that look right but don't get used. The first 30 days create the foundation that makes everything in days 31 to 90 defensible and relevant, not just plausible.
